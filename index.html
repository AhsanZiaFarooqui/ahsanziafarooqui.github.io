About

Public finance, state capacity, and evidence-based policy design

I am a PhD candidate in Economics at the University of Sussex and a Doctoral Fellow at the International Centre for Tax and Development (ICTD) and the Local Government Revenue Initiative (LoGRI). I am also a Graduate Fellow at the Institute for Development and Economic Alternatives (IDEAS), Pakistan.

My research sits at the intersection of public finance and state capacity, with a focus on how frontline bureaucrats' discretion shapes policy outcomes in Pakistan. Methodologically, I combine administrative and survey data with causal inference tools—including field experiments and quasi-experimental designs—and use institutional detail to link empirical estimates to policy design. I have led research projects on policing, national and subnational taxation (property and excise systems), digital public infrastructure, and public-school teacher recruitment. A recurring theme is testing how technology can reduce discretionary distortions and improve citizens' perceptions of fairness in government services.

Community Policing Does Not Build Citizen Trust in Police or Reduce Crime in the Global South

G. Blair, J. Weinstein, F. Christia, E. Arias, E. Badran, R.A. Blair, A. Cheema, A. Farooqui, T. Fetzer, G. Grossman, D. Haim, Z. Hameed, et al. · Science, 2021

A coordinated set of six randomized field experiments in Brazil, Colombia, Liberia, Pakistan, the Philippines, and Uganda reaching ~9 million people, finding that standard community policing interventions do not improve trust or reduce crime.
